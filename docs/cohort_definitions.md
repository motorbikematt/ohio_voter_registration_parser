# Ohio Voter Cohorts: Simple Definitions and Guide

This document explains the seven voter cohorts (groups) used to analyze Ohio voter behavior. 

---

## Part 1: Quick Definitions (Legend Style)
*Useful for website legends and quick reference.*

*   **Affiliated Voters** (Voters who have participated in a primary within the past two calendar years, establishing an official party status):
    *   **Purists** (Consistent party primary voters who have never voted in the opposing party's primary):
        *   **Pure D:** Democratic-affiliated voters who have never voted in a Republican primary (includes inactive voters who have never crossed over).
        *   **Pure R:** Republican-affiliated voters who have never voted in a Democratic primary (includes inactive voters who have never crossed over).
    *   **Mixed – Active:** Affiliated voters (Republican or Democratic) who have crossed over to participate in both party primaries historically.
*   **Unaffiliated Voters (UNC)** (Voters with no official party status under R.C. 3513.19 because they did not vote in a partisan primary during the current and preceding two calendar years, or have never voted in a primary):
    *   **Lapsed Purists** (Behavioral partisans who are currently unaffiliated, but have only voted in one party's primary historically):
        *   **UNC – Lapsed D:** Unaffiliated voters who have voted in Democratic primaries but never Republican.
        *   **UNC – Lapsed R:** Unaffiliated voters who have voted in Republican primaries but never Democratic.
    *   **Mixed – Lapsed:** Unaffiliated voters who have crossed over to participate in both party primaries historically (or have only voted in non-partisan local issue primaries).
    *   **UNC – No Primary:** Unaffiliated voters who have never participated in any primary election on record.

## Part 2: Understanding the Cohorts (A Complete Primer)
*A standalone guide explaining how these cohorts work, why we use them, and their limits.*

### How Party Affiliation Works in Ohio
To understand these groups, you first need to know how Ohio voting laws work. 

In many states, you sign up (register) for a political party when you register to vote. **Ohio does not allow this.** 

When you register to vote in Ohio, you are registering only as a general voter, not as a member of any party. To vote in any election, you must be registered at least 30 days before that election. 

Under Ohio law, party affiliation **only** happens at the polling station on primary election day. You affiliate with a party by showing up to vote in a primary and requesting that party's ballot (for example, asking for the Republican primary ballot). You cannot choose a party online or on your voter registration form.

Furthermore, this party affiliation does not last forever. Under **Ohio Revised Code (R.C.) Sections 3513.19 and 3513.05**, a voter's party affiliation is legally determined by their primary voting history in the **current calendar year and the two immediately preceding calendar years**. 

If you do not vote in a partisan primary during this three-year window, your party affiliation automatically drops off, and you return to being officially unaffiliated. Because of this, looking only at a voter's current, official status gives a very incomplete picture of how they actually behave.

### Why We Use These Cohorts (Their Utility)
Instead of looking only at a voter's current, official status, our system analyzes their **26-year voting history** across all primaries. This allows us to group voters into the seven cohorts. 

These cohorts are highly useful for several reasons:
1.  **Finding Consistent Partisans:** "Pure R" and "Pure D" voters are highly loyal. They have never crossed party lines in a primary, making them the base of their respective parties.
2.  **Identifying Aligned Independents:** "Lapsed R" and "Lapsed D" voters are officially unaffiliated, but their behavior shows they are loyal primary voters for one party. They are excellent targets for campaigns because they are already aligned but might not show up on basic party-member lists.
3.  **Flagging Crossover/Swing Voters:** "Mixed – Active" and "Mixed – Lapsed" voters have voted in primaries for both parties. These are true swing voters or strategic voters who require different messages.
4.  **Measuring Engagement:** "UNC – No Primary" voters have never participated in a primary. This is the largest group in many counties and represents voters who are either unengaged or only participate in general elections.

### What They Are Not (Their Limitations)
While these cohorts are powerful, they have important limits:
*   **They do not show how often someone votes:** The "Pure" cohorts only tell us that a voter has never voted in the opposite party's primary. They do not tell us how often they actually vote. A "Pure R" voter who has voted in every primary for 10 years looks exactly the same on the chart as someone who voted in only one primary recently and skipped all the others.
*   **They are not general election predictions:** A general election ballot is secret. Just because a voter participates in a Republican primary does not guarantee they will vote for the Republican candidate in the general election.
*   **Primary voting can be strategic:** Sometimes, a loyal Democrat will request a Republican ballot in a local primary to vote for a moderate school board member or local sheriff. This "crossover" vote is recorded, but it does not mean the voter has changed their political beliefs.
*   **They rely only on primary history:** If a voter only votes in November general elections, they will be classified as "UNC – No Primary," even if they have voted for the same party in every November election for decades.
*   **They do not tell us "why":** The data tells us *what* ballot a voter selected, but it cannot tell us the voter's motivations or personal beliefs.
