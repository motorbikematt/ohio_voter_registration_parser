# Ohio Voter Cohorts: Simple Definitions and Guide

This document explains the seven voter cohorts (groups) used to analyze Ohio voter behavior. 

---

## Part 1: Quick Definitions (Legend Style)
*Useful for website legends and quick reference.*

*   **Pure R (Pure Republican):** Voters officially affiliated with the Republican Party who have never voted in a Democratic primary.
*   **UNC – Lapsed R (Unaffiliated – Lapsed Republican):** Voters who are currently unaffiliated, have voted in Republican primaries in the past, but have never voted in a Democratic primary.
*   **Mixed – Active:** Voters officially affiliated with a party (Republican or Democratic) who have voted in primaries for both parties over the years.
*   **Mixed – Lapsed:** Voters who are currently unaffiliated and have voted in primaries for both parties in the past (or only voted in non-partisan local issue primaries).
*   **UNC – No Primary (Unaffiliated – No Primary History):** Voters who are currently unaffiliated and have never voted in any primary election on record.
*   **UNC – Lapsed D (Unaffiliated – Lapsed Democrat):** Voters who are currently unaffiliated, have voted in Democratic primaries in the past, but have never voted in a Republican primary.
*   **Pure D (Pure Democrat):** Voters officially affiliated with the Democratic Party who have never voted in a Republican primary.

## Part 2: Understanding the Cohorts (A Complete Primer)
*A standalone guide explaining how these cohorts work, why we use them, and their limits.*

### How Party Affiliation Works in Ohio
To understand these groups, you first need to know how Ohio voting laws work. 

In many states, you register as a Democrat or Republican when you sign up to vote. **Ohio does not do this.** When you register in Ohio, you are listed as an unaffiliated voter. 

You only become affiliated with a party when you show up to vote in a primary election and request a specific party's ballot (for example, asking for the Republican primary ballot). 

Furthermore, this status does not last forever. If you do not vote in a primary for two calendar years, your party status "lapses." You officially become unaffiliated again. Because of this, looking only at a voter's current registration status gives a very incomplete picture of how they actually behave.

### Why We Use These Cohorts (Their Utility)
Instead of looking only at a voter's current, official status, our system analyzes their **26-year voting history** across all primaries. This allows us to group voters into the seven cohorts. 

These cohorts are highly useful for several reasons:
1.  **Finding Consistent Partisans:** "Pure R" and "Pure D" voters are highly loyal. They have never crossed party lines in a primary, making them the base of their respective parties.
2.  **Identifying Aligned Independents:** "Lapsed R" and "Lapsed D" voters are officially unaffiliated, but their behavior shows they are loyal primary voters for one party. They are excellent targets for campaigns because they are already aligned but might not show up on basic party-member lists.
3.  **Flagging Crossover/Swing Voters:** "Mixed – Active" and "Mixed – Lapsed" voters have voted in primaries for both parties. These are true swing voters or strategic voters who require different messages.
4.  **Measuring Engagement:** "UNC – No Primary" voters have never participated in a primary. This is the largest group in many counties and represents voters who are either unengaged or only participate in general elections.

### What They Are Not (Their Limitations)
While these cohorts are powerful, they have important limits:
*   **They are not general election predictions:** A general election ballot is secret. Just because a voter participates in a Republican primary does not guarantee they will vote for the Republican candidate in the general election.
*   **Primary voting can be strategic:** Sometimes, a loyal Democrat will request a Republican ballot in a local primary to vote for a moderate school board member or local sheriff. This "crossover" vote is recorded, but it does not mean the voter has changed their political beliefs.
*   **They rely only on primary history:** If a voter only votes in November general elections, they will be classified as "UNC – No Primary," even if they have voted for the same party in every November election for decades.
*   **They do not tell us "why":** The data tells us *what* ballot a voter selected, but it cannot tell us the voter's motivations or personal beliefs.
